üá∫üá∏ America First Policy

What is "America First"?

Definition: A nationalist approach to foreign policy that prioritizes American national interest, security, and economic benefit above international cooperation and multilateral commitments.

Core Principles:

1. National Interest First All foreign policy decisions made with American advantage in mind, not global considerations
2. Bilateral Over Multilateral Direct country-to-country negotiations preferred over international organizations like the UN or WTO
3. Military Strength "Peace through Strength" - belief that military capability prevents conflict
4. Trade Balance Focus Reducing U.S. trade deficits through tariffs and negotiated agreements
5. Alliance Burden-Sharing Allies must pay fair share for their own defense, not rely on U.S. security umbrella
6. Sovereignty Over International Rules U.S. should not be bound by international institutions that limit American freedom of action

üìä Trade Wars and Tariff Policies

What are Tariffs?

Tariff Definition: A tax imposed on imported goods to protect domestic industries from foreign competition. Higher tariffs make foreign products more expensive, encouraging consumers to buy domestic products.

Steel and Aluminum Tariffs (2018)

Date Implemented: March 1, 2018
Steel Tariff: 25% on all imported steel
Aluminum Tariff: 10% on all imported aluminum
Legal Basis: Section 232 of Trade Expansion Act of 1962 (national security justification)

Trump's Tariff Escalation Over Time

Trump announced doubled steel tariffs to 50% in June 2025

2025 Escalation - Doubled Tariffs

In June 2025, Trump announced plans to double tariffs:
  • Steel: 25% ‚Üí 50%
  • Aluminum: 10% ‚Üí 25%
  • Reciprocal tariffs: 10%-100% on various countries

The China Trade War

Justification: Alleged unfair Chinese trade practices - intellectual property theft, forced technology transfer, currency manipulation
Scope: Tariffs on $370+ billion of Chinese goods, rates exceeding 25%
Trade Deficit Concern: U.S. trade deficit with China exceeded $200 billion annually
Retaliation: China imposed counter-tariffs on U.S. agricultural products, steel, chemicals
The U.S.-China trade war affected global supply chains

Who Got Hurt?

Consumers: Higher prices on imported goods - electronics, clothing, appliances
Farmers: Retaliatory tariffs on U.S. agricultural exports (soybeans, corn)
Manufacturers: Small businesses using imported materials faced higher production costs
Allies: Canada, EU, Mexico faced tariffs even though they're U.S. allies

Who Benefited?

Domestic Steel Industry: Reduced foreign competition and higher prices for U.S. steel
Domestic Aluminum Industry: Protected market share from foreign imports
Manufacturing Regions: Some job growth in steel and aluminum production

üõ°Ô∏è NATO: Pressure on Allies and Defense Spending

What is NATO?

NATO (North Atlantic Treaty Organization): Military alliance of 32 countries that provides mutual defense. Founded in 1949, it guarantees that an attack on one member is an attack on all (Article 5).

Trump's Main Complaint: "NATO Members Don't Pay Enough"

Problem Trump Identified: Most NATO members weren't spending 2% of their GDP on defense as agreed in 2014
Trump's View: U.S. was subsidizing European defense and other members were getting a "free ride" on American military strength
His Solution: Pressure NATO members to increase defense spending significantly
NATO defense spending varied widely among member states

Results of Trump's Pressure Campaign

Metric Before Trump (2014) After Trump (2024) Members meeting 2% target 3 of ~30 members 23 of 32 members Average NATO defense spending <2% of GDP 2.2% of GDP New target (2025) 2% of GDP 5% of GDP by 2035

The Article 5 Controversy

Article 5: NATO's core principle - an armed attack on any member is an attack on all. This mutual defense commitment is the foundation of NATO.
Trump's Controversial Statements: He suggested that U.S. support for Article 5 would depend on whether member countries met spending targets. This created uncertainty about the foundation of NATO.
Later Clarification: Trump eventually clarified U.S. commitment to Article 5 while maintaining pressure on spending increases.

2025 NATO Summit Achievement

In June 2025, NATO members agreed to increase defense spending to 5% of GDP annually by 2035‚Äîmore than double the previous target. This was viewed as a major victory for Trump's pressure campaign.

üå± Paris Climate Agreement Withdrawal

What is the Paris Climate Agreement?

Paris Agreement: An international accord signed by 195 nations in 2015 to combat global warming by reducing greenhouse gas emissions. Each country sets its own emission reduction targets.

Trump's Withdrawal Announcement

Date Announced: June 1, 2017
Date Effective: November 4, 2020 (one day after 2020 election)
Famous Quote: "I was elected to represent the citizens of Pittsburgh, not Paris"
Trump announced U.S. withdrawal from Paris Climate Agreement in June 2017

Trump's Stated Reasons for Withdrawal

Reason 1: "Draconian" environmental regulations would hurt American businesses and workers
Reason 2: Unfair burden - developed nations like the U.S. face stricter rules than developing nations like China and India
Reason 3: Economic costs to American workers outweighed climate benefits
Reason 4: U.S. reduced emissions while growing economically, so the Paris commitment was unnecessary

Global Reaction

Criticism from:
  • Environmental scientists and organizations
  • International leaders (especially French President Macron)
  • Businesses that supported climate action
  • Pittsburgh Mayor (ironically, he contradicted Trump, saying 80% of Pittsburgh voters favored climate action)
The U.S. withdrawal weakened global climate action efforts

Consequences

Impact:
  • ‚Ä¢ U.S. lost leadership position on climate change
  • ‚Ä¢ Gave China opportunity to position itself as climate leader
  • ‚Ä¢ Weakened global climate action momentum
  • ‚Ä¢ Dismantled climate-related regulations and initiatives
Recent Development: In January 2025, Trump signed Executive Order reinitiating the Paris Agreement withdrawal process in his second term.

üë• Immigration Restrictions: H-1B Visas

What is the H-1B Visa?

H-1B Visa: A temporary work visa that allows U.S. companies to employ foreign nationals in specialized occupations requiring college degrees (e.g., computer programmers, engineers, doctors, consultants).

Why Are H-1B Visas Important?

For Companies: Allows recruitment of highly skilled workers when domestic talent is unavailable
For Workers: Offers qualified international professionals pathway to U.S. employment and potential immigration
For Innovation: Enables tech companies and research institutions to access global talent

Trump's Policy: Restrictions and Suspensions

2018-2019: Administrative Tightening

H-1B Denial Rate Increase: Rose from ~15% (pre-Trump) to 24% (FY 2018) and 21% (FY 2019)
Proposed Changes: Switch from lottery system to wage-based selection prioritizing higher-paid workers
Effect: More applications rejected, fewer visas issued

June 2020: Temporary Suspension

Action: Trump suspended entry of H-1B, L-1, H-2B, and J-1 visa holders through December 2020
Justification: COVID-19 pandemic economic impact, job protection for Americans
Impact: Prevented ~525,000 foreign workers from entering the U.S.

Trump's Stated Rationale

Argument: H-1B program was exploited to replace American workers with cheaper foreign labor, suppressing American wages.

Real-World Impact

Technology Sector: Companies faced difficulty recruiting specialized talent (AI, software engineering)
Brain Drain: International students and professionals pursued opportunities in Canada, Europe, India
Innovation Slowdown: Reduced global talent flow affected R&D capabilities of U.S. tech companies
Supporters' View: Protects American jobs and ensures wages aren't suppressed by cheaper foreign labor

Special Impact on India

India is the largest source of H-1B visa holders. Approximately 60-70% of H-1B visas go to Indian nationals. Trump's restrictions disproportionately affected Indian tech workers and outsourcing companies.

üåê Impact on Globalization

What is Globalization?

Globalization: The process of increasing interconnection of economies, cultures, and people worldwide through trade, technology, investment, and communication.

Trump's Challenge to Globalization

From Multilateralism to Economic Nationalism

Globalization Model (Pre-Trump) Trump's Economic Nationalism Reduced trade barriers worldwide Strategic tariffs and trade restrictions Most-Favored-Nation equal treatment Bilateral deals with selective benefits WTO-centered dispute resolution Unilateral U.S. action bypassing WTO Foreign investment encouraged Review and restrict foreign ownership Global supply chains prioritized Domestic production and "reshoring"

Rise of Protectionism

Protectionism: Government policies that restrict imports and protect domestic industries from foreign competition through tariffs, quotas, and trade agreements.
Result: Higher prices for consumers, reduced global trade, supply chain disruptions

Weakening of Multilateral Institutions

World Trade Organization (WTO)

Trump's Critique: WTO enabled China to get "away with murder" by allowing it developing nation status despite economic size
Action Taken: Blocked appointment of judges to WTO Appellate Body, paralyzing dispute resolution mechanism
Result: WTO unable to resolve trade disputes, weakening its authority and credibility

Supply Chain Disruptions

Before Trump: Global supply chains optimized for efficiency and lowest cost
During Trump: Tariff uncertainty caused companies to diversify suppliers and consider domestic alternatives
Consequence: Higher production costs, slower innovation, reduced economic efficiency

Economic Effects on Consumers

Higher Prices: Tariffs passed to consumers through higher prices on imported goods - electronics, clothing, appliances
Supply Shortages: Companies struggled to source materials, leading to delayed products and availability issues
Job Uncertainty: While some domestic manufacturing jobs created, uncertainty about future tariffs deterred new investment

Did Globalization End?

Answer: No, but it transformed. Globalization didn't end but changed its form:
Competitive Globalization
Countries actively protecting their own industries and competing for advantage
Nationalist Globalization
Trade and investment tied to national interest rather than pure economics
Regional Blocs
Shift toward regional trade agreements (USMCA) over global frameworks
De-Globalization Trends
Companies "reshoring" production back to home countries for security

Global Trade Uncertainty (Still Present in 2025)

Trump's unpredictable tariff approach continues creating uncertainty:
  • Constant threat of new tariffs deters long-term business investment
  • Exchange rate volatility increases financial uncertainty
  • Companies maintain higher inventory to buffer against supply disruptions
  • Global corporations diversify suppliers geographically to reduce risk

Impact on Global Trade System

Shift from Multilateralism to Economic Nationalism:

Trump replaced cooperation-based trade with competition-based trade. Instead of WTO rules binding all countries equally, Trump pursued bilateral deals and unilateral tariff policies. This weakened the post-WWII international order and created uncertainty in global commerce.

Is This Comparable to the 1930s?

Economic Historians' Warning: Trump's 2025 tariff escalation (50% on steel, reciprocal tariffs up to 100%) resembles the 1930s Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act, which triggered global retaliatory tariffs and deepened the Great Depression. Sustained protectionism risks similar global economic consequences.

Final Thought

Trump's policies showed that one country's choice to withdraw from global cooperation can reshape international relations. Whether "America First" strengthened the U.S. or weakened the global system remains debated, but its impact on trade, alliances, and multilateral institutions is undeniable.
